Chitgar Symphony

Master's Thesis - Green Corridor Design

The Creation of an Ecologically-Oriented Urban Corridor: Enhancing Ecological Urban Branding and Biodiversity Preservation through integrating Chitgar Lake, Chitgar Park, and the Botanical Garden in Tehran, Iran.

This Master's thesis project at Tarbiat Modares University creates a tangible representation of Tehran's dedication to ecological sustainability. The green corridor design incorporates educational interpretation elements, informative signage, interactive displays, and educational paths to enhance community engagement and environmental awareness.

Key features include vertical gardens, living walls, indigenous plant species, multi-functional recreational spaces, cultural event venues, and children's learning gardens. The project focuses on habitat connectivity, biodiversity conservation, and establishing a unique ecological urban character that promotes community ownership and environmental stewardship.

Supervised by Dr. Medi Haghighat Bin with consultation from Professor Mohammad Reza Pourjafar.

Pedestrian Serenade

Urban Design - Pedestrian Street Revitalization

Pedestrian Serenade: Revitalizing Vanak Village through Pedestrian-Oriented Design

This landscape design project at Tarbiat Modares University centers on creating a vibrant pedestrian street in Vanak Village, Tehran, Iran. Led by Dr. Mehdi Haghighat Bin, the project seeks to rejuvenate the urban structure through pedestrian-oriented design while preserving the district's distinct cultural legacy.

Key Design Elements: Pedestrian-focused infrastructure with expanded sidewalks, designated pedestrian areas, and clearly marked crosswalks. The design incorporates seating areas, public art installations, pocket parks, and indigenous plant species to create an inclusive environment that fosters social interaction and supports local businesses.

Cultural Integration: The design method emphasizes understanding and conservation of Vanak Village's distinctive essence, incorporating traditional aesthetic elements that create seamless connection between the pedestrian street and surrounding built environment. Features include designated areas for open-air markets, artisanal product stalls, and cultural gatherings to support the local economy.

The project focuses on preserving and rehabilitating the indigenous ecosystem, identifying vulnerable habitats, and enhancing biodiversity through habitat restoration and sustainable land use practices. Neckar Nexus: The Neckar as a Mediator

Collaborative Urban Planning - Social Inclusion & Community Integration

Neckar Nexus: The Neckar as a Mediator - Bridging Communities Through River-Centered Urban Planning

Colleagues: Habiba Al Harrasi, Gréta Fazekas, and Veronika Babiča

Supervised by Dr. Shaharin Elham, Angie Carolina Camacho Gutiérrez, Dr. Astrid Ley, and Dr. Leonie Fischer.

Research Methodology: We conducted extensive field research including semi-structured interviews, surveys, and public life observations, particularly focusing on the Arab community in Bad Cannstatt. Our research revealed challenges of time scarcity, emotional distance from the river, and cultural gaps, while identifying opportunities for intercultural exchange and community building.

Strategic Framework: Our three-phase approach centers on Social Support, Diversity & Inclusion, and Social Cohesion & Trust. Phase 1 maps community encounters and needs; Phase 2 activates connections through co-designed cultural events and workshops; Phase 3 leverages the Neckar as a long-term mediator through sustainable community spaces and digital platforms.

It Takes a Village to Raise a Child

Educational Architecture - Community-Centered School Design

"It Takes a Village to Raise a Child" - Community-Centered Educational Architecture

Colleagues: Silvia Ruiz, Shraddha Adhikari, and Ayesha Parveen

This architectural project reimagines educational spaces as organic extensions of village life, beginning not with walls or classrooms, but with the child and the community around them. The design philosophy centers on creating a "structure for belonging" that grows naturally with the village rhythm.

Design Concept: The school unfolds as five flexible learning units arranged around a central courtyard - the heart of the design where learning flows through open air, where rain falls and dries, where sounds echo and children play. Each unit serves as a space for exploring, learning, and growing, adaptable for lessons, storytelling, or circular play.

Community Integration: The design features three essential layers: the inner nurturing space, the permeable shaded walkways for communication between instructors and caregivers, and the "community edge" - an invitation rather than a barrier. This edge bends where people linger and widens where they gather, shaped not by the architect's hand but by the village's footsteps.

Organic Growth: The architecture allows for natural expansion - a kitchen for village meals, a library with local stories, additional classrooms, and celebration spaces emerge as needed. The school doesn't transform the village; the village shapes the school, creating a rhythm of belonging that remains forever open to growth.
